Lemma 6.2. Explicit involution-orbit cocycle.

Let \(H\) be a finite group, let \(g\in H\) have order \(2\text{,}\) and let \(M=\F_2[H]\) with left-regular action
\begin{equation*} (c x)_h=x_{c^{-1}h}. \end{equation*}
Choose a set \(\mathcal R\subset H\) of representatives for the right cosets \(u\langle g\rangle\text{.}\) For \(c\in H\) and \(u\in\mathcal R\text{,}\) write uniquely
\begin{equation} c^{-1}u=\pi_c(u)g^{\epsilon_c(u)}, \qquad \pi_c(u)\in\mathcal R, \quad \epsilon_c(u)\in\F_2.\tag{6.9} \end{equation}
Define
\begin{align} q_g(x)&=\sum_{u\in\mathcal R}x_u x_{ug},\tag{6.10}\\ f_g(x,y)&=\sum_{u\in\mathcal R}x_u y_{ug},\tag{6.11}\\ m_c^g(x)&=\sum_{u\in\mathcal R} \epsilon_c(u)x_{\pi_c(u)}x_{\pi_c(u)g}.\tag{6.12} \end{align}
Then
\begin{align} m_c^g(x+y)+m_c^g(x)+m_c^g(y) &=f_g(cx,cy)+f_g(x,y),\tag{6.13}\\ m_{cd}^g(x)&=m_c^g(dx)+m_d^g(x), \qquad m_1^g=0.\tag{6.14} \end{align}
Consequently
\begin{equation} \kappa_g^{\mathcal R}((x,c),(y,d)) =f_g(x,cy)+m_c^g(y)\tag{6.15} \end{equation}
is a normalized \(2\)-cocycle on \(M\rtimes H\text{.}\) It vanishes on the zero section \(H\hookrightarrow M\rtimes H\text{,}\) and its restriction to \(M\) has square map \(q_g\text{.}\)

Proof.
We first compute the transition functions for the right-coset action, then verify the quadratic correction, and finally apply the general equivariant extension formula.
🔗
From (6.9) one obtains
\begin{equation} \pi_{cd}=\pi_d\circ\pi_c, \qquad \epsilon_{cd}(u)=\epsilon_c(u)+\epsilon_d(\pi_c(u)).\tag{6.16} \end{equation}
Moreover,
\begin{equation*} f_g(cx,cy) =\sum_{u\in\mathcal R} x_{\pi_c(u)g^{\epsilon_c(u)}} y_{\pi_c(u)g^{\epsilon_c(u)+1}}. \end{equation*}
For \(\epsilon_c(u)=0\) this is the summand of \(f_g(x,y)\) indexed by \(\pi_c(u)\text{;}\) for \(\epsilon_c(u)=1\) its orientation is reversed. Therefore
\begin{equation*} f_g(cx,cy)+f_g(x,y) =\sum_{u\in\mathcal R}\epsilon_c(u) \bigl(x_{\pi_c(u)}y_{\pi_c(u)g} +x_{\pi_c(u)g}y_{\pi_c(u)}\bigr), \end{equation*}
which is exactly the polarization of (6.12). This proves (6.13).
🔗
The product \(x_vx_{vg}\) is unchanged when \(v\) is replaced by \(vg\text{.}\) Hence
\begin{equation*} m_c^g(dx) =\sum_{u\in\mathcal R}\epsilon_c(u) x_{\pi_d(\pi_c(u))}x_{\pi_d(\pi_c(u))g}. \end{equation*}
Adding \(m_d^g(x)\) and using (6.16) gives (6.14). The cocycle assertion now follows directly from Lemma 6.1. Finally, \(f_g(x,x)=q_g(x)\) and \(m_1^g=0\text{,}\) proving the restriction and normalization claims.
