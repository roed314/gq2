Lemma 5.13. Normal forms for nontrivial simple modules.

Let \(V\) be a nontrivial simple tame \(\F_2[C]\)-module. Every class in \(H^1_{A,\rho}(V)\) has a unique representative supported on \(\notn{x0}{x_0}\text{:}\)
\begin{equation} (a,b,c,d)\sim(0,0,c,0).\tag{5.24} \end{equation}
More precisely, the two possible inertia cases are as follows.
🔗
  1. If \(\mathsf T=1\text{,}\) then \(P=1\text{,}\) \(1+\mathsf S^{-1}\) is invertible, and
    \begin{equation*} Z^1_{A,\rho}(V)=\{(a,0,c,0):a,c\in V\},\qquad B^1_{A,\rho}(V)=\{((\mathsf S-1)v,0,0,0):v\in V\}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
  2. If \(V^{\mathsf T}=0\text{,}\) then \(P=0\) and \(\mathsf T-1=1+\mathsf T\) is invertible. The wild row first forces \(d=0\text{.}\) Subtracting the coboundary of \(v=(\mathsf T-1)^{-1}b\) kills \(b\text{,}\) and the tame row then forces \(a=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
Under the representatives (5.24), the degree-one pairing with the corresponding representative \(\lambda\in V^\vee\) is
\begin{equation} (c,\lambda)\longmapsto \begin{cases} \lambda(c),&\mathsf T=1,\\[1mm] \lambda((1+\mathsf U+\mathsf U^{-1})c),&V^{\mathsf T}=0. \end{cases}\tag{5.25} \end{equation}
Both operators in (5.25) are invertible.
🔗

Proof.
We reduce separately in the cases of trivial inertia and vanishing inertia fixed space, and then evaluate the pairing on the resulting unique normal forms.
🔗
Because tame inertia is normal, \(V^{\mathsf T}\) is a \(C\)-submodule; simplicity gives either \(\mathsf T=1\) and \(P=1\text{,}\) or \(V^{\mathsf T}=0\) and \(P=0\text{.}\)
🔗
If \(\mathsf T=1\text{,}\) the two rows of (5.4) reduce to \(b=0\) and \((1+\mathsf S^{-1})d=0\text{.}\) A nonzero kernel of \(1+\mathsf S^{-1}\) would be the \(\mathsf S\)-fixed subspace and hence a nonzero submodule, so \(1+\mathsf S^{-1}\) is invertible and \(d=0\text{.}\) The displayed descriptions of \(Z^1\) and \(B^1\) follow. The same argument shows that \(\mathsf S-1\) is invertible, so each class has a unique representative \((0,0,c,0)\text{.}\)
🔗
If \(V^{\mathsf T}=0\text{,}\) then \(1+\mathsf T\) is invertible. The wild row is \(\mathsf S^{-1}d=0\text{,}\) hence \(d=0\text{.}\) For a cocycle \((a,b,c,0)\text{,}\) subtract the coboundary of \(v=(\mathsf T-1)^{-1}b\text{.}\) The new representative has \(b=0\text{,}\) and its tame row is \(\mathsf S^{-1}(1+\mathsf T)a=0\text{,}\) so \(a=0\text{.}\) Uniqueness follows because a coboundary with zero \(\mathsf T\)-coordinate has \((\mathsf T-1)v=0\text{,}\) hence \(v=0\text{.}\)
🔗
The pairing formula is Lemma 5.14. In the ramified case \(\mathsf U\) has \(2\)-power order, so its minimal polynomial divides a power of \(X+1\text{.}\) Since \(X^2+X+1\) is coprime to \(X+1\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} 1+\mathsf U+\mathsf U^{-1}=\mathsf U^{-1}(\mathsf U^2+\mathsf U+1) \end{equation*}
is invertible. The unramified operator is the identity.
