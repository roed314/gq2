Lemma 10.1. Exhaustion by tame boundary frames.

Let \(G\) be finite and put \(L=O_2(G)\text{.}\) For either source \(\notnfar{GammaSrc}{\Gamma}\text{,}\) every epimorphism \(f:\notnfar{GammaSrc}{\Gamma}\twoheadrightarrow G\) determines a unique tame boundary frame
\begin{equation*} \notnfar{TA}{\TA}\twoheadrightarrow G/L. \end{equation*}
Conversely, with decoration \(\notnfar{Etarget}{E}=0\text{,}\) a boundary-framed epimorphism to \(G\) with a fixed tame frame is exactly an ordinary epimorphism to \(G\) inducing that frame. Distinct tame frames give disjoint sets of epimorphisms.

Proof.
The characteristic marked wild subgroup maps into \(O_2(G)=L\text{,}\) so every epimorphism has a well-defined quotient map to \(G/L\text{.}\) By Proposition 3.2, this quotient map is a tame frame. Conversely, when \(\notnfar{Etarget}{E}=0\) there is no extra decoration, and the boundary-framed condition records only the induced tame quotient map and the marked \(2\)-kernel. Thus a boundary-framed epimorphism with target \(G\) is precisely an ordinary epimorphism inducing the chosen frame. The induced quotient map \(\notnfar{TA}{\TA}\to G/L\) is unique, so the fixed-frame sets are disjoint as the frame varies.
