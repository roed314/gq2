Proposition 5.15. Candidate deformation duality.

For every elementary \(\F_2[C]\)-module \(A\text{,}\) the chain map (5.17) is a quasi-isomorphism. Hence
\begin{equation} H^2_{A,\rho}(A)^\vee\cong (A^\vee)^C, \qquad \dim Z^1_{A,\rho}(A)=2\dim A+\dim(A^\vee)^C.\tag{5.27} \end{equation}
The induced pairings
\begin{equation*} H^i_{A,\rho}(A)\times H^{2-i}_{A,\rho}(A^\vee)\to\F_2 \end{equation*}
are perfect and natural in \(A\text{.}\)

Proof.
We treat the trivial simple module, then the nontrivial simple modules, and finally pass to arbitrary elementary modules by exact cone d\’evissage.
🔗
For \(A=\F_2\text{,}\) all lower actions are trivial, so \(d^0=0\) and the actual Jacobian is
\begin{equation*} d^1(a,b,c,d)=(b,b). \end{equation*}
Thus \(\dim H^0=1\text{,}\) \(\dim H^1=3\text{,}\) and \(\dim H^2=1\text{.}\) On the basis of \(H^1\) represented by the coordinates \((\notn{sigma}{\sigma},\notn{x0}{x_0},\notnfar{x1}{x_1})\text{,}\) the traced Fox–Heisenberg form is
\begin{equation*} ((a,c,d),(a',c',d'))\longmapsto ad'+da'+cc', \end{equation*}
with matrix
\begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} 0&0&1\\ 0&1&0\\ 1&0&0 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
It is nonsingular, and the endpoint trace gives a nonzero \(H^0\)–\(H^2\) pairing. This is exactly the scalar cup–Bockstein table (3.18).
🔗
For a nontrivial simple module, Lemma 5.12 makes the action tame, and Lemma 5.13 gives a unique degree-one representative supported on \(\notn{x0}{x_0}\text{.}\) The Jacobian is surjective, so \(H^0=H^2=0\) and \(\dim H^1=\dim A\text{.}\) On the unique normal forms the degree-one pairing is exactly (5.25), and is nonsingular by the final assertion of Lemma 5.13. Hence the cone \(K(A)\) is acyclic for every simple module, including the trivial simple module treated above.
🔗
For a general elementary module, choose a composition series. The degreewise short exact cone sequence (5.21) and its long exact cohomology sequence give acyclicity at each extension step. Thus \(D_{\rho,A}\) is a quasi-isomorphism for every elementary, possibly nonsplit and nonsemisimple, module \(A\text{.}\) The numerical formula follows from duality and the Euler characteristic of (5.1).
