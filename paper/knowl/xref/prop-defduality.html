Proposition 5.15. Candidate deformation duality.

For every elementary \(\F_2[\notn{Cfin}{C}]\)-module \(\notn{Amod}{A}\text{,}\) the chain map (5.17) is a quasi-isomorphism. Hence
\begin{equation} H^2_{A,\notnfar{rhoEpi}{\rho}}(\notn{Amod}{A})^\vee\cong (\notn{Amod}{A}^\vee)^\notn{Cfin}{C}, \qquad \dim Z^1_{A,\notnfar{rhoEpi}{\rho}}(\notn{Amod}{A})=2\dim \notn{Amod}{A}+\dim(\notn{Amod}{A}^\vee)^\notn{Cfin}{C}.\tag{5.27} \end{equation}
The induced pairings
\begin{equation*} H^i_{A,\notnfar{rhoEpi}{\rho}}(\notn{Amod}{A})\times H^{2-i}_{A,\notnfar{rhoEpi}{\rho}}(\notn{Amod}{A}^\vee)\to\F_2 \end{equation*}
are perfect and natural in \(\notn{Amod}{A}\text{.}\)

Proof.
We treat the trivial simple module, then the nontrivial simple modules, and finally pass to arbitrary elementary modules by exact cone dévissage.
🔗
For \(\notn{Amod}{A}=\F_2\text{,}\) all lower actions are trivial, so \(d^0=0\) and the actual Jacobian is
\begin{equation*} d^1(a,b,c,d)=(b,b). \end{equation*}
Thus \(\dim H^0=1\text{,}\) \(\dim H^1=3\text{,}\) and \(\dim H^2=1\text{.}\) On the basis of \(H^1\) represented by the coordinates \((\notn{sigma}{\sigma},\notn{x0}{x_0},\notnfar{x1}{x_1})\text{,}\) the traced Fox–Heisenberg form is
\begin{equation*} ((a,c,d),(a',c',d'))\longmapsto ad'+da'+cc', \end{equation*}
with matrix
\begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} 0&0&1\\ 0&1&0\\ 1&0&0 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*}
It is nonsingular, and the endpoint trace gives a nonzero \(H^0\)–\(H^2\) pairing. This is exactly the scalar cup–Bockstein table (3.18).
🔗
For a nontrivial simple module, Lemma 5.12 makes the action tame, and Lemma 5.13 gives a unique degree-one representative supported on \(\notn{x0}{x_0}\text{.}\) The Jacobian is surjective, so \(H^0=H^2=0\) and \(\dim H^1=\dim \notn{Amod}{A}\text{.}\) On the unique normal forms the degree-one pairing is exactly (5.25), and is nonsingular by the final assertion of Lemma 5.13. Hence the cone \(K(\notn{Amod}{A})\) is acyclic for every simple module, including the trivial simple module treated above.
🔗
In more detail: in the split case, fixed-point-freeness of \(\mathsf S-1\) makes this operator bijective and supplies the unique \(x_0\)-supported normal form on both \(A\) and \(A^\vee\text{.}\) In the ramified case the analogous argument uses \(\mathsf T^{-1}-1\text{,}\) while the perfect pairing is reduced to the bijectivity of \(1+\mathsf U+\mathsf U^{-1}\text{.}\) Its injectivity comes from the unipotence of the \(2\)-primary operator \(\mathsf U\text{;}\) finiteness then gives surjectivity.
🔗
For a general elementary module, choose a composition series. The degreewise short exact cone sequence (5.21) and its long exact cohomology sequence give acyclicity at each extension step. Thus \(D_{\rho,\notn{Amod}{A}}\) is a quasi-isomorphism for every elementary, possibly nonsplit and nonsemisimple, module \(\notn{Amod}{A}\text{.}\) The numerical formula follows from duality and the Euler characteristic of (5.1).
🔗
The dévissage is a strong induction on \(|A|\text{,}\) rather than an appeal to semisimplicity. If \(A\) is neither zero nor simple, choose a proper nonzero \(C\)-stable subgroup \(W\text{.}\) Both \(W\) and \(A/W\) have strictly smaller cardinality, inherit the exponent-two action, and fit into the exact cone sequence of Lemma 5.11. The two-out-of-three property for that sequence therefore propagates duality through arbitrary nonsplit extensions.
