Proposition 6.18. Dyadic base determinant theorem.

If \(d=\dim V\text{,}\) then
\begin{equation} \#(Q^0_{\mathrm{loc}})^{-1}(0)= \begin{cases} 2^{d-1}-2^{d/2-1},&V\text{ unramified},\\ 2^{d-1}+2^{d/2-1},&V\text{ ramified}. \end{cases}\tag{6.57} \end{equation}
Equivalently, the base local Gauss sum is negative in the unramified case and positive in the ramified case. The candidate base form \(Q_A^0\) has the same Gauss sum by Proposition 6.9.

Proof.
The ramified and unramified cases are treated separately: the first follows from the half-dimensional totally singular subspace, while the second is determined by the invariant Hermitian-line classification.
🔗
In the ramified case, local Euler–Poincar\’e and Tate duality give \(\dim H^1(\notn{Qtwo}{\Qtwo},V)=d\text{.}\) By Lemma 6.17, the nonsingular base local quadratic space contains a totally singular subspace of dimension \(d/2\text{,}\) so it is hyperbolic.
🔗
In the unramified case, the faithful image is cyclic of odd order. Put \(F_V=\End_{H_V}(V)\text{.}\) Self-duality equips \(F_V\cong\F_{2^{2m}}\) with the involution \(x\mapsto x^*=x^{2^m}\) and fixed field \(F_{V,0}\cong\F_{2^m}\text{.}\) Inflation–restriction and the local Euler characteristic identify \(H^1(\notn{Qtwo}{\Qtwo},V)\) with a one-dimensional \(F_V\)-space. The norm-one group
\begin{equation*} \mathcal U=\{u\in F_V^\times:uu^*=1\} \end{equation*}
preserves the polar form of \(q\text{.}\) For \(u\in\mathcal U\text{,}\) the function \(x\mapsto q(ux)+q(x)\) is linear and \(H_V\)-invariant. Since \((V^\vee)^{H_V}=0\text{,}\) it vanishes; hence every \(u\in\mathcal U\) is an \(H_V\)-equivariant \(q\)-isometry. By (6.21), \((u\rtimes1)^*[\kappa_q^0]=[\kappa_q^0]\text{.}\) Functoriality of graph pullback therefore gives
\begin{equation*} Q^0_{\mathrm{loc}}(u_*x)=Q^0_{\mathrm{loc}}(x) \qquad(u\in\mathcal U). \end{equation*}
Thus Lemma 6.7 applies to the base local form, and it is
\begin{equation*} Q(x)=\Tr_{F_{V,0}/\F_2}(a xx^*), \qquad a\in F_{V,0}^\times. \end{equation*}
The norm map \(F_V^\times\to F_{V,0}^\times\) has fibres of size \(2^m+1\text{.}\) Among the nonzero elements of \(F_{V,0}\text{,}\) exactly \(2^{m-1}-1\) have trace \(0\text{.}\) Hence
\begin{equation*} \#Q^{-1}(0)=1+(2^m+1)(2^{m-1}-1) =2^{2m-1}-2^{m-1}, \end{equation*}
which is the minus-type number in (6.57).
