    David Roe

    Abstract
    We give an explicit profinite presentation of the absolute Galois group \(G_{\mathbb{Q}_2}\text{.}\) The presentation has four generators \(\notn{sigma}{\sigma},\notn{tau}{\tau},\notn{x0}{x_0},\notn{x1}{x_1}\text{;}\) the closed normal subgroup generated by \(\notn{x0}{x_0},\notn{x1}{x_1}\) is required to be pro-\(2\text{;}\) and there are two defining relations. The first is the tame relation \(\notn{tau}{\tau}^\notn{sigma}{\sigma}=\notn{tau}{\tau}^2\text{.}\) The second, involving the pro-\(2\) generators, is the explicit relation stated in Theorem 1.2. This is a dyadic counterpart to the known four-generator presentations of \(G_{\mathbb{Q}_p}\) for odd primes \(p\text{.}\) The case \(p=2\) requires a separate argument because the odd-prime formula contains the exponent \((p+1)/2\text{,}\) which is not integral at \(p=2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The proof first identifies the tame quotient and the maximal pro-\(2\) quotient of the proposed group, together with their common map to the unramified quotient \(\mathbb{Z}_2\text{.}\) The maximal pro-\(2\) quotient is identified with the rank-three Demushkin group attached to \(\mathbb{Q}_2\) using Labute’s classification, local reciprocity, and a lifting theorem for automorphisms of its abelianization. We then compare lifting problems from the proposed group and from \(G_{\mathbb{Q}_2}\) through extensions with finite elementary abelian \(2\)-group kernels. On the presentation side these lifting problems are controlled by an explicit three-term complex obtained from the two defining relators; on the Galois side they are controlled by continuous cohomology and local Tate duality. For self-dual simple \(\mathbb{F}_2\)-modules, the lifting count contains an additional quadratic obstruction; we compute it from the dyadic filtration of square classes and an explicit formula for the cohomological norm in a quadratic extension. Finite Fourier inversion and induction on the order of the normal \(2\)-subgroup in a finite target then give equality of epimorphism counts to every finite group. A profinite reconstruction argument yields the asserted presentation.

    Section 1 Introduction and main theorem

    This section fixes the conventions that determine the two displayed relators, states the presentation theorem, and isolates the marked local normalization needed in its proof. The final subsection reduces the remaining argument to a comparison of finite epimorphism counts.
    🔗
    For odd primes \(p\text{,}\) the absolute Galois group of \(\mathbb{Q}_p\) admits an explicit four-generator presentation of Jannsen–Wingberg type; see [1, Chapter VII]. The dyadic case is exceptional because the odd-prime formula uses an exponent containing \((p+1)/2\text{.}\) We give a separate dyadic relation.
    🔗
    Throughout, exponentiation by an element of \(\notn{Zhat}{\Zhat}\) has its usual profinite meaning. Let
    \begin{equation*} \notn{w}{\w}=(1\text{ in }\mathbb{Z}_2,\;0\text{ in }\mathbb{Z}_\ell\text{ for every odd prime }\ell). \end{equation*}
    Thus \(\notn{w}{\w}^2=\notn{w}{\w}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We use the conventions
    \begin{equation*} x^g=g^{-1}xg, \qquad [x,y]=x^{-1}y^{-1}xy. \end{equation*}
    The unramified coordinate is normalized geometrically throughout: if \(\Phi_{\mathrm{ar}}\) is arithmetic Frobenius and \(\Phi_{\mathrm{geom}}=\Phi_{\mathrm{ar}}^{-1}\text{,}\) then \(\notn{nuur}{\nu_{\mathrm{ur}}}(\Phi_{\mathrm{geom}})=1\text{.}\) With the right-conjugation convention above, a standard tame generator satisfies
    \begin{equation*} \notn{tau}{\tau}^{\Phi_{\mathrm{geom}}}=\notn{tau}{\tau}^q. \end{equation*}
    Thus the displayed relation \(\notn{tau}{\tau}^\notn{sigma}{\sigma}=\notn{tau}{\tau}^2\) means that \(\notn{sigma}{\sigma}\) maps to geometric Frobenius. Put
    \begin{equation} \sigma_2=\notn{sigma}{\sigma}^{\notn{w}{\w}},\qquad u_i=(x_i\notn{tau}{\tau})^{\notn{w}{\w}}\;(i=0,1),\qquad d_0=u_0\notn{x0}{x_0}^{-1},\tag{1.1} \end{equation}

    The proof is given in Sections 3.1–3.3.

    Theorem 1.2. Presentation theorem.

    The absolute Galois group \(\notn{GQ}{\GQ}\) is isomorphic to the profinite group topologically generated by
    \begin{equation*} \notn{sigma}{\sigma},\notn{tau}{\tau},\notn{x0}{x_0},\notn{x1}{x_1}, \end{equation*}
    for which the closed normal subgroup generated by \(\notn{x0}{x_0},\notn{x1}{x_1}\) is pro-\(2\text{,}\) and which is subject to the two relations
    \begin{equation} \notn{tau}{\tau}^\notn{sigma}{\sigma}=\notn{tau}{\tau}^2\tag{1.5} \end{equation}
    and
    \begin{equation} h_0u_1^{-1}\notn{x1}{x_1}^\notn{sigma}{\sigma} c_0=1,\tag{1.6} \end{equation}
    where the auxiliary words are defined in (1.1)–(1.3).

    Explicit presentations of this kind have a substantial history. For odd \(p\text{,}\) the structure of the full group \(G_{\mathbb{Q}_p}\) by generators and relations is due to Jannsen and Wingberg [12]; see [1, Chapter VII] for a systematic account. In residue characteristic two, Zel’venskiĭ [13] described the Galois group of the maximal \(2\)-extension for \(2\)-adic base fields of odd degree, and Diekert [14] obtained structure results for absolute Galois groups of dyadic fields under additional hypotheses. Presentations of local Galois groups also feed anabelian questions about their automorphisms; see for instance [16].

    Subsection 1.1 Strategy of the proof

    The proof has six stages.
    🔗
    1. The candidate \(\notn{GA}{\GA}\) is constructed from its admissible finite quotients. A reconstruction lemma reduces the theorem to equality of epimorphism counts from \(\notn{GA}{\GA}\) and \(\notn{GQ}{\GQ}\) to every finite group.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. The tame quotient of \(\notn{GA}{\GA}\) is identified with the tame quotient of \(\notn{GQ}{\GQ}\text{.}\) Its maximal pro-\(2\) quotient is identified with \(G_{\notn{Qtwo}{\Qtwo}}(2)\) in a form preserving the full unramified character to \(\mathbb{Z}_2\text{,}\) not merely its reduction modulo \(2\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. The tame and maximal pro-\(2\) maps are combined over their common unramified quotient. Fixing this fibre-product datum turns the comparison into a finite lifting problem with a prescribed tame quotient and a prescribed map to an elementary abelian \(2\)-group.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. For extensions with elementary abelian \(2\)-group kernel, lifts from the candidate are governed by the two defining relators, while lifts from \(\notn{GQ}{\GQ}\) are governed by continuous cohomology. The two theories are shown to have the same obstruction dimensions, lift multiplicities, duality pairings, and connecting maps.
      🔗
      🔗
    5. When a simple coefficient module is self-dual, a scalar central pushout produces a quadratic obstruction. An exact word calculation on the candidate side and the dyadic square-class filtration on the local side give the same quadratic Gauss sum.
      🔗
      🔗
    6. For a finite target, the normal \(2\)-subgroup is reduced through an elementary quotient and a central Frattini layer. Fourier inversion and the partition of homomorphisms according to their image subgroup reduce the count to strictly smaller targets. Strong induction gives equality of all finite epimorphism counts, and profinite reconstruction gives the required isomorphism.
      🔗
      🔗
    The dependence of the argument is therefore
    \begin{equation*} \begin{gathered} \text{marked tame and maximal pro-$2$ quotients} \\ \Downarrow \\ \text{common tame--pro-$2$ data over $\mathbb{Z}_2$} \\ \Downarrow \\ \text{linear lifting comparison and quadratic obstruction comparison} \\ \Downarrow \\ \text{induction on the normal $2$-subgroup of a finite target} \\ \Downarrow \\ |\Sur(\notn{GA}{\GA},G)|=|\Sur(\notn{GQ}{\GQ},G)|\quad\text{for every finite group $G$} \\ \Downarrow \\ \notn{GA}{\GA}\cong\notn{GQ}{\GQ}. \end{gathered} \end{equation*}

    Section 2 The candidate as a profinite group

    Starting from the four generators, the two relators, and the requirement on the normal closure of \(\notn{x0}{x_0},\notn{x1}{x_1}\text{,}\) we construct the candidate as an inverse limit of finite quotients. The section ends with an exact finite-quotient semantics for this construction and the reconstruction criterion used in Section 10.
    🔗

    Subsection 2.1 The marked quotient construction

    Let \(F\) be the free profinite group on \(\notn{sigma}{\sigma},\notn{tau}{\tau},\notn{x0}{x_0},\notn{x1}{x_1}\text{.}\) The finite quotients relevant to the presentation are precisely those in which the four distinguished elements generate, satisfy both relators, and have \(2\)-group wild normal closure. Formally, call an epimorphism
    \begin{equation*} \varphi:F\twoheadrightarrow G \end{equation*}
    to a finite group admissible if the distinguished tuple generates \(G\text{,}\) satisfies (1.5) and (1.6), and the normal closure of \(\varphi(\notn{x0}{x_0}),\varphi(\notn{x1}{x_1})\) is a \(2\)-group. Let \(\mathcal Q_A\) be the class of all admissible finite quotients and set
    \begin{equation} N_A=\bigcap_{\varphi\in\mathcal Q_A}\ker\varphi, \qquad \notn{GA}{\GA}=F/N_A.\tag{2.1} \end{equation}
    Let \(\notn{WA}{\WA}\) denote the closed normal subgroup of \(\notn{GA}{\GA}\) generated by \(\notn{x0}{x_0},\notn{x1}{x_1}\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Lemma 2.1. Finite subdirect closure.

    If \(F\twoheadrightarrow G_i\) are finitely many admissible quotients and \(Y\) is the image of \(F\) in \(\prod_iG_i\text{,}\) then \(F\twoheadrightarrow Y\) is admissible and its kernel is the intersection of the given kernels.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.
    The two relations hold coordinatewise. The distinguished tuple generates \(Y\text{.}\) The normal closure of \(\notn{x0}{x_0},\notn{x1}{x_1}\) in \(Y\) lies in
    \begin{equation*} Y\cap\prod_i O_2(G_i), \end{equation*}
    which is a finite \(2\)-group. Since \(Y\) is the image of the diagonal map \(F\to\prod_iG_i\text{,}\) one has
    \begin{equation*} \ker(F\to Y)=\bigcap_i\ker(F\to G_i), \end{equation*}
    which proves the assertion about kernels.
    🔗
    🔗

    Lemma 2.2. Cofinality.

    Every finite quotient of \(\notn{GA}{\GA}\) is an admissible marked quotient. In particular, \(\notn{WA}{\WA}\) is pro-\(2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.
    Let \(K\triangleleft_{\mathrm{open}}F\) contain \(N_A\text{.}\) For every \(g\notin K\text{,}\) the definition of \(N_A\) gives an admissible quotient whose kernel does not contain \(g\text{.}\) Compactness of \(F\setminus K\) gives finitely many such kernels whose intersection is contained in \(K\text{.}\) By Lemma 2.1, this intersection is itself the kernel of an admissible quotient. Therefore \(F/K\) is a quotient of an admissible quotient, and admissibility passes to quotients. The last assertion follows because every finite image of \(\notn{WA}{\WA}\) is a \(2\)-group.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 2.3. Epimorphic finite-quotient semantics.

    For every finite group \(G\text{,}\) epimorphisms \(\notn{GA}{\GA}\twoheadrightarrow G\) are in natural bijection with generating quadruples \((\notn{sigma}{\sigma},\notn{tau}{\tau},\notn{x0}{x_0},\notn{x1}{x_1})\in G^4\) satisfying (1.5), (1.6), and \(\notn{x0}{x_0},\notn{x1}{x_1}\in O_2(G)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Lean (equivalence)Lean (main)
    🔗

    Proof.
    An epimorphism sends \(\notn{WA}{\WA}\) to a normal \(2\)-subgroup of \(G\text{,}\) hence into \(O_2(G)\text{.}\) Conversely, a generating quadruple with \(\notn{x0}{x_0},\notn{x1}{x_1}\in O_2(G)\) has normal \(2\)-group closure and defines an admissible quotient in \(\mathcal Q_A\text{.}\)

    Subsection 2.2 A reconstruction lemma

    Lemma 2.5. One-sided profinite reconstruction.

    Let \(P\) be a topologically finitely generated profinite group and let \(Q\) be any profinite group. If
    \begin{equation*} |\Sur(P,H)|=|\Sur(Q,H)| \end{equation*}
    for every finite group \(H\text{,}\) then \(P\cong Q\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Lean (from equal counts)Lean (main)
    🔗

    Proof.
    We construct an epimorphism in each direction from compatible finite quotients and then use Hopficity to identify both maps as isomorphisms.
    🔗
    Since \(P\) is finitely generated, \(\Sur(P,H)\) and hence \(\Sur(Q,H)\) are finite for every finite \(H\text{.}\) The two groups have exactly the same finite quotients.
    🔗
    For \(n\ge1\text{,}\) let \(R_n(P)\) be the intersection of the kernels of all maps from \(P\) to groups of order at most \(n\text{,}\) and put \(P_n=P/R_n(P)\text{.}\) The \(P_n\) form a cofinal inverse system of finite quotients of \(P\text{.}\) Every \(P_n\) is a quotient of \(Q\text{.}\) For any finite set of indices, a sufficiently large \(P_m\) maps onto all corresponding \(P_n\text{;}\) composing an epimorphism \(Q\twoheadrightarrow P_m\) gives a compatible family on that finite set. Compactness of the inverse limit of the finite nonempty sets \(\Sur(Q,P_n)\) yields a compatible family and therefore an epimorphism \(Q\twoheadrightarrow P\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Interchanging the two groups, use the directed system of all finite quotients of \(Q\text{.}\) The sets of epimorphisms from \(P\) to those quotients are finite and have the finite intersection property, so there is an epimorphism \(P\twoheadrightarrow Q\text{.}\) The composite \(P\twoheadrightarrow Q\twoheadrightarrow P\) is a surjective endomorphism of a finitely generated profinite group. Such groups are Hopfian; see, for example, [4, Section 2.5]. Hence the composite is an isomorphism, and so is \(P\twoheadrightarrow Q\text{.}\)
    🔗
    In more detail: the counting hypothesis is used only through finite data. Summing surjection counts over the subgroups of a finite \(H\) converts the hypothesis into \(|\Hom(P,H)|=|\Hom(Q,H)|\) for every finite \(H\text{,}\) and these counts are finite because \(P\) is topologically finitely generated. The formalization also assumes \(Q\) topologically finitely generated: with counts encoded as natural numbers, an infinite homomorphism set would make the count hypothesis vacuous rather than false, so finiteness on both sides is part of the statement’s content. In the application this is harmless — the candidate is finitely generated by construction, and the finite generation of \(\GQ\) is exactly the foundational input B1.
    🔗
    🔗
    The topological finite generation of \(\notn{GQ}{\GQ}\) required in this argument is classical; see [1, Theorem (7.5.14)] or [12]. Reconstruction of profinite groups from their finite quotients goes back to [17]; the counting refinement used here is a one-sided variant.

    Section 3 The tame and maximal pro-\(2\) quotients

    The candidate construction of Section 2 is now compared with the corresponding tame and maximal pro-\(2\) quotients of \(G_{\notn{Qtwo}{\Qtwo}}\text{.}\) Besides the abstract quotient identifications, we determine compatible maps to the full unramified quotient \(\mathbb{Z}_2\text{;}\) those marked maps are the input to the fibre product in Section 4.

    Let
    \begin{equation*} \notn{TA}{\TA}=\langle \notn{sigma}{\sigma},\notn{tau}{\tau}\mid \notn{tau}{\tau}^\notn{sigma}{\sigma}=\notn{tau}{\tau}^2\rangle_{\mathrm{prof}} \end{equation*}

    Lemma 3.1. Finite tame quotients.

    In every finite quotient generated by \(s,t\) with \(t^s=t^2\text{,}\) the element \(t\) has odd order, and the quotient has the form
    \begin{equation*} C_e\rtimes C_n, \qquad e\text{ odd}, \qquad s^{-1}ts=t^2. \end{equation*}
    Every normal \(2\)-subgroup of such a quotient is central and lies in the unramified cyclic direction.
    🔗
    Lean (odd order)Lean (central 2-subgroup)
    🔗

    Proof.
    Conjugation preserves order, whereas \(|t^2|=|t|/\gcd(2,|t|)\text{,}\) so \(|t|\) is odd. Let \(I=\langle t\rangle\) and suppose \(sI\) has order \(n\text{.}\) Then \(s^n=t^m\text{.}\) Since \(s^n\) commutes with \(s\text{,}\) the relation \(t^s=t^2\) gives \(t^m=t^{2m}\text{,}\) hence \(m\equiv0\pmod{|t|}\text{.}\) Thus the extension splits.
    🔗
    If \(N\) is a normal \(2\)-subgroup, then \(N\cap I=1\) and \([N,I]\le N\cap I=1\text{.}\) Hence \(N\) centralizes \(I\text{.}\) It is therefore contained in the \(2\)-primary part of the kernel of the Frobenius action on \(I\text{,}\) which is central and unramified.

    There are canonical isomorphisms
    \begin{equation*} \notn{GA}{\GA}/\notn{WA}{\WA}\cong \notn{TA}{\TA}\cong \notn{GQ}{\GQ}/\notn{WF}{\WF}, \end{equation*}

By Lemma 3.1, \(\notn{tau}{\tau}\) has odd order in every finite quotient, so \(\notn{tau}{\tau}^\notn{w}{\w}=1\text{.}\) Hence \(u_i=d_0=z_0=c_0=d_g=h_c=h_0=1\text{;}\) the words \(\sigma_2\) and \(g_0\) may remain nontrivial, but they only conjugate the identity. The wild relation therefore disappears.     Lemma 3.3. The wild subgroup is the characteristic \(2\)-core.

    The tame group \(\notn{TA}{\TA}\) has no nontrivial closed normal pro-\(2\) subgroup. Consequently
    \begin{equation*} O_2(\notn{GA}{\GA})=\notn{WA}{\WA}, \qquad O_2(\notn{GQ}{\GQ})=\notn{WF}{\WF}. \end{equation*}
    In particular, the marked wild subgroups of the two sources are characteristic.
    🔗
    Lean (trivial images)Lean (maximality)
    🔗

    Proof.
    Let \(I=\overline{\langle\notn{tau}{\tau}\rangle}\triangleleft\notn{TA}{\TA}\) be tame inertia; it is pro-odd. If \(N\triangleleft\notn{TA}{\TA}\) is pro-\(2\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} N\cap I=1, \qquad [N,I]\le N\cap I=1, \end{equation*}
    so \(N\) centralizes \(I\text{.}\) Its image in \(\notn{TA}{\TA}/I\cong\widehat{\mathbb{Z}}\) lies in the \(2\)-primary factor and has a \(2\)-adic geometric Frobenius coordinate \(a\in\mathbb{Z}_2\text{.}\) For every \(m\ge1\text{,}\) use the finite inertia quotient of order
    \begin{equation*} e_m=2^{2^m}-1. \end{equation*}

    Remark 3.6. Why the orientation cannot be omitted.

    An un-oriented marked-relator normalization with only the abelianized relation and quadratic initial form is false. Let \(F=\langle A,S,\notn{Ygen}{Y}\rangle_{\mathrm{pro}-2}\) and define

    Subsection 5.2 The Fox–Heisenberg cup calculation

    For an elementary module \(A\text{,}\) set

    Subsection 6.1 The normalized equivariant base class

    A \(C\)-invariant quadratic form on \(V\) need not admit a \(C\)-invariant factor set for a chosen section \(V\to E\text{.}\) The correction terms in the lifted \(C\)-action are therefore included in the equivariant central extension class.
    🔗

    Lemma 6.1. Equivariant extraspecial connecting cocycle.

    Let \(V\) be an elementary \(\F_2[C]\)-module, let \(q:V\to\F_2\) be a \(C\)-invariant quadratic form with polar form \(b_q\text{,}\) and let
    \begin{equation*} f:V\times V\to\F_2, \qquad f(v,v)=q(v),\qquad f(v,w)+f(w,v)=b_q(v,w), \end{equation*}
    be a normalized factor set for the central extension
    \begin{equation*} E_f=V\times\F_2, \qquad (v,z)(w,t)=(v+w,z+t+f(v,w)). \end{equation*}
    When an equivariant lift is chosen, its action in the normalized section is written by functions \(m_c:V\to\F_2\) satisfying
    \begin{equation} m_c(v+w)+m_c(v)+m_c(w)=f(cv,cw)+f(v,w)\tag{6.1} \end{equation}
    and
    \begin{equation} m_{cd}(v)=m_c(dv)+m_d(v),\qquad m_1=0.\tag{6.2} \end{equation}
    Equivalently,
    \begin{equation*} c\cdot(v,z)=(cv,z+m_c(v)) \end{equation*}
    defines an action by automorphisms of \(E_f\text{.}\) The associated normalized base central cocycle on \(V\rtimes C\) is
    \begin{equation} \kappa_q^0((v,c),(w,d))=f(v,cw)+m_c(w).\tag{6.3} \end{equation}
    Consequently, for a source \(\Gamma\text{,}\) a lower map \(\rho:\Gamma\to C\text{,}\) and a normalized \(V\)-valued \(1\)-cocycle \(b\text{,}\) the base connecting cocycle is
    \begin{equation} (b,\rho)^*\kappa_q^0(g,h) =f(b(g),\rho(g)b(h))+m_{\rho(g)}(b(h)).\tag{6.4} \end{equation}
    🔗
    If \(\kappa\) is another normalized central cocycle on \(V\rtimes C\) whose restriction to \(V\) is the same quadratic form \(q\text{,}\) then, after replacing \(\kappa\) by a cohomologous normalized cocycle and changing the normalized section, the following is an equality of normalized \(2\)-cochains:
    \begin{equation} \kappa=\kappa_q^0+\Gamma_\gamma+ \operatorname{inf}_C^{V\rtimes C}\delta,\tag{6.5} \end{equation}
    where
    \begin{equation} \Gamma_\gamma((v,c),(w,d))=\gamma(c)(cw), \qquad \gamma\in Z^1(C,V^\vee),\quad \delta\in Z^2(C,\F_2).\tag{6.6} \end{equation}
    After graph pullback and application of the source functional \(\iota_\Gamma\text{,}\) the corresponding scalar quadratic obstruction is
    \begin{equation} Q_{\kappa,\Gamma,\rho}(b) =Q^0_{\Gamma,\rho}(b) +\langle b,\rho^*\gamma\rangle_\Gamma +\iota_\Gamma(\rho^*\delta),\tag{6.7} \end{equation}
    where
    \begin{equation*} Q^0_{\Gamma,\rho}(b)= \iota_\Gamma\bigl((b,\rho)^*\kappa_q^0\bigr). \end{equation*}
    Its polarization is the source pairing transported by \(b_q\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.
    We verify the equivariant central extension, identify the filtration-one and inflated difference terms, and then compute the polarization.
    🔗
    Equation (6.1) is exactly the condition that \((v,z)\mapsto(cv,z+m_c(v))\) preserve the multiplication in \(E_f\text{;}\) equation (6.2) says that these automorphisms form a genuine \(C\)-action. Multiplication in the semidirect product attached to this lifted action is
    \begin{equation} (v,c,z)(w,d,t)= (v+cw,cd,z+t+f(v,cw)+m_c(w)),\tag{6.8} \end{equation}
    which gives (6.3). Pulling back this cocycle by the graph of a \(1\)-cocycle \(b\) gives (6.4). This is the term which would be missing if one incorrectly assumed that the section \(v\mapsto(v,0)\) were \(C\)-equivariant.
    🔗
    For the difference statement, the quotient \(\kappa-\kappa_q^0\) restricts trivially to \(V\times V\text{.}\) In the normalized Lyndon–Hochschild–Serre filtration for the split extension \(1\to V\to V\rtimes C\to C\to1\text{,}\) such a class has only a filtration-one part in \(Z^1(C,V^\vee)\) and an inflated scalar part in \(Z^2(C,\F_2)\text{.}\) The normalized representative is exactly (6.6); the cocycle condition for it is the condition that \(\gamma\) be a \(1\)-cocycle. Pullback along \((b,\rho)\) gives (6.7). Polarization of the base term is obtained by adding the formulas for \(b+b'\text{,}\) \(b\text{,}\) and \(b'\text{;}\) the \(m_c\)-contribution cancels because each \(m_c\) has polar form \(f(c-,c-)+f(-,-)\text{,}\) and the remaining term is \(b_q(b\smile b')\text{.}\) The difference terms in (6.7) are linear or inflated scalar terms and therefore do not change the polar form.

    Lemma 6.2. Explicit involution-orbit cocycle.

    Let \(H\) be a finite group, let \(g\in H\) have order \(2\text{,}\) and let \(M=\F_2[H]\) with left-regular action
    \begin{equation*} (c x)_h=x_{c^{-1}h}. \end{equation*}
    Choose a set \(\mathcal R\subset H\) of representatives for the right cosets \(u\langle g\rangle\text{.}\) For \(c\in H\) and \(u\in\mathcal R\text{,}\) write uniquely
    \begin{equation} c^{-1}u=\pi_c(u)g^{\epsilon_c(u)}, \qquad \pi_c(u)\in\mathcal R, \quad \epsilon_c(u)\in\F_2.\tag{6.9} \end{equation}
    Define
    \begin{align} q_g(x)&=\sum_{u\in\mathcal R}x_u x_{ug},\tag{6.10}\\ f_g(x,y)&=\sum_{u\in\mathcal R}x_u y_{ug},\tag{6.11}\\ m_c^g(x)&=\sum_{u\in\mathcal R} \epsilon_c(u)x_{\pi_c(u)}x_{\pi_c(u)g}.\tag{6.12} \end{align}
    Then
    \begin{align} m_c^g(x+y)+m_c^g(x)+m_c^g(y) &=f_g(cx,cy)+f_g(x,y),\tag{6.13}\\ m_{cd}^g(x)&=m_c^g(dx)+m_d^g(x), \qquad m_1^g=0.\tag{6.14} \end{align}
    Consequently
    \begin{equation} \kappa_g^{\mathcal R}((x,c),(y,d)) =f_g(x,cy)+m_c^g(y)\tag{6.15} \end{equation}
    is a normalized \(2\)-cocycle on \(M\rtimes H\text{.}\) It vanishes on the zero section \(H\hookrightarrow M\rtimes H\text{,}\) and its restriction to \(M\) has square map \(q_g\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.
    We first compute the transition functions for the right-coset action, then verify the quadratic correction, and finally apply the general equivariant extension formula.
    🔗
    From (6.9) one obtains
    \begin{equation} \pi_{cd}=\pi_d\circ\pi_c, \qquad \epsilon_{cd}(u)=\epsilon_c(u)+\epsilon_d(\pi_c(u)).\tag{6.16} \end{equation}
    Moreover,
    \begin{equation*} f_g(cx,cy) =\sum_{u\in\mathcal R} x_{\pi_c(u)g^{\epsilon_c(u)}} y_{\pi_c(u)g^{\epsilon_c(u)+1}}. \end{equation*}
    For \(\epsilon_c(u)=0\) this is the summand of \(f_g(x,y)\) indexed by \(\pi_c(u)\text{;}\) for \(\epsilon_c(u)=1\) its orientation is reversed. Therefore
    \begin{equation*} f_g(cx,cy)+f_g(x,y) =\sum_{u\in\mathcal R}\epsilon_c(u) \bigl(x_{\pi_c(u)}y_{\pi_c(u)g} +x_{\pi_c(u)g}y_{\pi_c(u)}\bigr), \end{equation*}
    which is exactly the polarization of (6.12). This proves (6.13).
    🔗
    The product \(x_vx_{vg}\) is unchanged when \(v\) is replaced by \(vg\text{.}\) Hence
    \begin{equation*} m_c^g(dx) =\sum_{u\in\mathcal R}\epsilon_c(u) x_{\pi_d(\pi_c(u))}x_{\pi_d(\pi_c(u))g}. \end{equation*}
    Adding \(m_d^g(x)\) and using (6.16) gives (6.14). The cocycle assertion now follows directly from Lemma 6.1. Finally, \(f_g(x,x)=q_g(x)\) and \(m_1^g=0\text{,}\) proving the restriction and normalization claims.

    Lemma 6.3. Base equivariant determinant class.

    Let \(V\) be a nontrivial simple self-dual tame \(\F_2[C]\)-module, let \(H=H_V\) be its faithful tame image, and let \(q:V\to\F_2\) be a nonzero \(H\)-invariant nonsingular quadratic form. There is a normalized base class
    \begin{equation*} \kappa_q^0\in Z^2(V\rtimes H,\F_2) \end{equation*}
    whose restriction to \(V\) has square map \(q\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Choose an \(H\)-split pair
    \begin{equation*} V\xrightarrow{i}W=\F_2[H]^N\xrightarrow{p}V, \qquad pi=1, \end{equation*}
    and put \(q_W=q\circ p\text{.}\) In the left-regular coordinates \(X_{j,h}\) of \(W\text{,}\) the unique algebraic normal form of the \(H\)-invariant quadratic map \(q_W\) is a sum of orbit polynomials of the following three types:
    \begin{align*} S_j&=\sum_{h\in H}X_{j,h}^2,\\ C_{j,k,g}&=\sum_{h\in H}X_{j,h}X_{k,hg} \quad\text{when the corresponding unordered pair has trivial stabilizer},\\ E_{j,g}&=\sum_{u\in\mathcal R_g}X_{j,u}X_{j,ug}, \qquad g^2=1,\ g\ne1, \end{align*}
    where \(\mathcal R_g\) is any set of representatives for the right cosets of \(\langle g\rangle\) in \(H\text{.}\) For a square orbit use the \(H\)-invariant factor set
    \begin{equation} f_{S_j}(x,y)=\sum_{h\in H}x_{j,h}y_{j,h}, \qquad m_c=0.\tag{6.17} \end{equation}
    For a free orbit use
    \begin{equation} f_{C_{j,k,g}}(x,y)=\sum_{h\in H}x_{j,h}y_{k,hg}, \qquad m_c=0.\tag{6.18} \end{equation}
    For an involution-stabilized orbit use the cocycle (6.15) of Lemma 6.2 on the \(j\)th regular summand, with \(\mathcal R=\mathcal R_g\text{.}\) Let \(\kappa_{q_W}^0\) be the sum of the cocycles corresponding to the orbit polynomials occurring in \(q_W\text{,}\) and put
    \begin{equation} \kappa_q^0=(i\rtimes1)^*\kappa_{q_W}^0.\tag{6.19} \end{equation}
    Then \(\kappa_q^0\) vanishes on the zero section and \(\operatorname{res}_V(\kappa_q^0)\) has square map \(q\text{.}\) If a lower target has quotient \(C\) acting through \(H\text{,}\) we inflate this class along \(C\to H\) and keep the same notation.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.
    We construct the class on a split regular module by the three possible orbit types of quadratic monomials and then pull it back to \(V\text{.}\)
    🔗
    If \(V\) is ramified, \(V\) is projective over \(\F_2[H]\) by Lemma 6.11; if \(V\) is unramified, the \(2\)-primary subgroup of the cyclic unramified image acts trivially on every simple characteristic-\(2\) module, so the faithful image \(H\) has odd order and Maschke’s theorem applies. Thus the split pair exists.
    🔗
    The algebraic normal form of a quadratic map over \(\F_2\) is determined by its values on the coordinate vectors and by its polar form. \(H\)-invariance makes its coefficients constant on the orbits of unordered degree-two coordinate monomials. Since the action on each regular coordinate set is free, the stabilizer of an unordered pair is either trivial or has order two and exchanges the two entries. The latter possibility is exactly \(j=k\) with a nontrivial involution \(g\text{,}\) and gives \(E_{j,g}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The factor sets (6.17) and (6.18) are \(H\)-invariant, vanish on the zero section, and have square maps \(S_j\) and \(C_{j,k,g}\text{,}\) respectively. By Lemma 6.2, the explicit cocycle for \(E_{j,g}\) has square map \(E_{j,g}\) and also vanishes on the zero section. Their sum is therefore an actual normalized equivariant central cocycle on \(W\rtimes H\) with fibre square map \(q_W\text{.}\) Pullback along \(i\rtimes1\) gives fibre square map \(q_W(iv)=q(v)\) and preserves zero-section normalization.

    Lemma 6.4. Uniqueness and isometry invariance of the normalized base class.

    Let \(V\text{,}\) \(H=H_V\text{,}\) and \(q\) be as in Lemma 6.3. Then
    \begin{equation} H^1(H,V^\vee)=0.\tag{6.20} \end{equation}
    Any two classes in \(H^2(V\rtimes H,\F_2)\) which restrict to the class with square map \(q\) on \(V\) and restrict trivially along the zero section \(H\hookrightarrow V\rtimes H\) are equal. Consequently, changing the normalized factor set, the correction functions \(m_c\text{,}\) the half-orbit transversals, or the \(H\)-split regular embedding and retraction changes \(\kappa_q^0\) only by a normalized coboundary. In particular the base quadratic obstruction \(Q^0_{\Gamma,\rho}\) is well-defined.
    🔗
    Moreover, every \(H\)-equivariant isometry \(u\) of \((V,q)\) fixes the normalized base class:
    \begin{equation} (u\rtimes1)^*[\kappa_q^0]=[\kappa_q^0].\tag{6.21} \end{equation}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.
    We first prove the required cohomology vanishing, then use the low-degree Lyndon–Hochschild–Serre sequence for uniqueness, and finally deduce choice-independence and isometry invariance.
    🔗
    If \(V\) is unramified, its faithful image \(H\) has odd order: the \(2\)-primary subgroup of the cyclic unramified image acts trivially on every simple characteristic-\(2\) module. Hence positive-degree \(\F_2[H]\)-cohomology vanishes by averaging.
    🔗
    Assume that \(V\) is ramified, and let \(I\triangleleft H\) be the odd tame-inertia subgroup. The fixed space \((V^\vee)^I\) is an \(H\)-submodule. It is zero, because \(V\) is simple and inertia acts nontrivially. Since \(|I|\) is odd, \(H^j(I,V^\vee)=0\) for \(j>0\text{.}\) The Hochschild–Serre spectral sequence for \(1\to I\to H\to H/I\to1\) therefore gives [5, Chapter VII]
    \begin{equation*} H^n(H,V^\vee)=0\qquad(n\ge0). \end{equation*}
    In particular (6.20) holds in both cases.
    🔗
    Put \(G=V\rtimes H\text{.}\) For an elementary abelian \(2\)-group \(V\text{,}\) the square-map correspondence
    \begin{equation*} H^2(V,\F_2)\xrightarrow{\sim} \{\text{quadratic maps }V\to\F_2\} \end{equation*}
    is an isomorphism: the polarization recovers the commutator form, and the square and commutator data determine the central extension class. Thus two classes with square map \(q\) have the same fibre restriction.
    🔗
    The low-degree Lyndon–Hochschild–Serre sequence for \(1\to V\to G\to H\to1\) contains the exact segment [5, Chapter VII]
    \begin{equation} H^2(H,\F_2)\xrightarrow{\operatorname{inf}} \ker\!\left(H^2(G,\F_2)\longrightarrow H^2(V,\F_2)\right) \xrightarrow{\operatorname{edge}}H^1(H,V^\vee).\tag{6.22} \end{equation}
    Let \(\xi\) be the difference of two classes with the stated normalizations. Its fibre restriction is zero. By (6.20) and exactness of (6.22), \(\xi=\operatorname{inf}(\delta)\) for some \(\delta\in H^2(H,\F_2)\text{.}\) Pullback along the zero section is a left inverse to inflation, while both classes vanish there. Hence \(\delta=0\) and \(\xi=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    At the filtration-one level one can see the same cancellation directly. If \(\gamma=dh\) for \(h\in V^\vee\text{,}\) then the normalized \(1\)-cochain
    \begin{equation} \ell_h(v,c)=h(v)\tag{6.23} \end{equation}
    on \(V\rtimes H\) satisfies \(d\ell_h=\Gamma_{dh}\text{,}\) with \(\Gamma\) as in (6.6).
    🔗
    If normalized cocycle representatives are used, their difference is \(da\) for a normalized \(1\)-cochain \(a\text{.}\) Since the difference vanishes on \(H\times H\text{,}\) \(a|_H\) is a \(1\)-cocycle. Subtracting its inflation makes \(a\) vanish on the zero section without changing \(da\text{.}\) Thus all choices in Lemma 6.3 give isomorphic normalized central extensions, and transport of lifts through that isomorphism leaves both the candidate relator obstruction and the local pullback class unchanged. This proves the choice-independence of \(Q^0_{\Gamma,\rho}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Finally, \((u\rtimes1)^*\kappa_q^0\) has the same fibre square map and the same zero-section restriction as \(\kappa_q^0\text{,}\) so uniqueness proves (6.21).
    🔗
    The uniqueness assertion is deliberately made over the faithful image \(H_V\text{.}\) After inflation to a larger quotient \(C\) acting through \(H_V\text{,}\) the group \(H^1(C,V^\vee)\) need not vanish; its contribution is precisely the filtration-one term \(\Gamma_\gamma\) in (6.5), and it remains part of the target-side affine data.

    🔗

    Proof.

    Appendix A Normalized cochains, Shapiro maps, corestriction, and the Evens norm

    The formulas in Lemma 6.13, Lemma 6.15 depend on choices of left action, coset representatives, and normalized sections. This appendix fixes those conventions at cochain level and gives the explicit normalized boundaries produced by every change of section or transversal.

    Subsection A.1 Normalized inhomogeneous cochains

    All coefficient modules are left modules. If \(G\) is profinite and \(M\) is a finite discrete continuous left \(G\)-module, then \(C^n(G,M)\) denotes the group of continuous normalized inhomogeneous \(n\)-cochains. Thus a cochain of positive degree vanishes whenever one of its arguments is \(1\text{,}\) and \(C^0(G,M)=M\text{.}\) In additive notation the differential is
    \begin{align} (df)(g_1,\ldots,g_{n+1}) &=g_1f(g_2,\ldots,g_{n+1}) +\sum_{i=1}^{n}(-1)^i f(g_1,\ldots,g_ig_{i+1},\ldots,g_{n+1})\notag\\ &\qquad+(-1)^{n+1}f(g_1,\ldots,g_n),\tag{A.1} \end{align}
    and, in degree zero,
    \begin{equation} (dm)(g)=gm-m.\tag{A.2} \end{equation}
    All coefficient groups in the normalization-sensitive calculations of Lemma 6.13, Lemma 6.15 are \(\F_2\)-vector spaces, so the minus signs in (A.1) disappear there.
    🔗
    Let \(\mu:A\times B\to C\) be a \(G\)-equivariant bilinear pairing of left \(G\)-modules. Our cup-product convention is
    \begin{equation} (f\smile g)(g_1,\ldots,g_{p+q}) =\mu\!\left( f(g_1,\ldots,g_p), (g_1\cdots g_p)g(g_{p+1},\ldots,g_{p+q}) \right).\tag{A.3} \end{equation}
    In particular, for trivial scalar coefficients, \((\alpha\smile\beta)(n_1,n_2)=\alpha(n_1)\beta(n_2)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We use the following three levels of assertion.
    🔗
    1. “Equal as normalized cochains” means equality in \(C^n(G,M)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. “Equal up to the displayed normalized coboundary” means \(f-g=dh\) for the particular normalized cochain \(h\) that is written down.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. “Equal in cohomology” means only \([f]=[g]\) in \(H^n(G,M)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    No cochain-level assertion in the main text is intended to pass silently from one of these levels to another.

    Subsection A.2 Conjugation and semidirect products

    The group-theoretic exponent convention remains
    \begin{equation*} x^g=g^{-1}xg. \end{equation*}
    It is distinct from the notation \(gm\) for the given left action of \(G\) on a module \(M\text{.}\) We write the semidirect product as \(M\rtimes G\text{,}\) with elements \((m,g)\) and multiplication
    \begin{equation} (m,g)(n,h)=(m+gn,gh), \qquad (m,g)^{-1}=(-g^{-1}m,g^{-1}).\tag{A.4} \end{equation}
    Consequently
    \begin{equation*} (0,g)(m,1)(0,g)^{-1}=(gm,1), \qquad (m,1)^{(0,g)}=(g^{-1}m,1). \end{equation*}
    Thus the manuscript’s right-conjugation notation acts on the normal module by \(g^{-1}\text{,}\) whereas the semidirect-product law uses the specified left action by \(g\text{.}\)
    🔗

    \begin{equation} \Phi_{b,\rho}:\Gamma\longrightarrow M\rtimes G, \qquad \gamma\longmapsto(b(\gamma),\rho(\gamma))\tag{A.5} \end{equation}
    is a homomorphism if and only if
    \begin{equation} b(\gamma\notnfar{eta}{\eta})=b(\gamma)+\rho(\gamma)b(\notnfar{eta}{\eta}).\tag{A.6} \end{equation}

    Subsection A.3 The left-regular coordinate convention

    For a finite group \(H\) we identify \(\F_2[H]\) with the functions \(x=(x_h)_{h\in H}\) and use the left-regular action

    \begin{equation*} (\bar\gamma x)_h=x_{\bar\gamma^{-1}h}. \end{equation*}

    \begin{equation} (\mathsf R_gx)_h=x_{hg}.\tag{A.8} \end{equation}
    The operators \(\mathsf R_g\) commute with the left-regular \(H\)-action. The monomial \(x_ux_{ug}\) is therefore naturally indexed by cosets written \(u\langle g\rangle\text{,}\) which explains the right multiplication by \(g\) and the coset convention in Lemma 6.2.

    Subsection A.4 The normalized Shapiro cochain map

    for which

    Thus the half-orbit change of transversal has a specified boundary, not only an abstract uniqueness argument.
    🔗
    Likewise, in the field-theoretic situation of Lemma 6.15, put
    \begin{equation*} \theta_\alpha =\bigl(\operatorname{Sh}_{K/K_0}(\alpha),\rho_0\bigr)^*\kappa_J \in Z^2(G_{K_0},\F_2) \end{equation*}
    and define \(\nu_{\alpha,\mathcal R}=\operatorname{cor}_{\mathcal R}\theta_\alpha\text{.}\) If the lifts of the representatives change from \(\widetilde u\) to \(\widetilde u'=\widetilde u a_u\) with \(a_u\in G_{K_0}\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation} \begin{split} \Lambda^\alpha_{\mathcal R,\mathcal R'}(\gamma) =\sum_{u\in\mathcal R}\bigl(& \theta_\alpha(a_u,\ell_u'(\gamma))\\ &+\theta_\alpha(\ell_u(\gamma),a_{\pi_{\bar\gamma}(u)}) \bigr) \end{split}\tag{A.33} \end{equation}
    satisfies
    \begin{equation} \nu_{\alpha,\mathcal R'}-\nu_{\alpha,\mathcal R} =d\Lambda^\alpha_{\mathcal R,\mathcal R'}.\tag{A.34} \end{equation}
    With the compatible nested transversals chosen in the proof of Lemma 6.15, equation (6.51) is a literal equality of normalized cochains. Equations (A.32) and (A.34) give the correction for arbitrary choices.
    🔗
    Finally, consider the index-two formula itself. If the lift \(\widetilde s\) is replaced by \(\widetilde s'=\widetilde s a\text{,}\) \(a\in N\text{,}\) then (A.25) gives \(b'=b+dq\) with \(q_1=0\) and \(q_s=\alpha(a)\text{.}\) Put \(A=(q,1)\in E\rtimes J\) and
    \begin{equation*} \Phi_b(\gamma)=(b(\gamma),\bar\gamma), \qquad \Phi_{b'}(\gamma)=(b'(\gamma),\bar\gamma). \end{equation*}
    Then
    \begin{equation*} \Phi_{b'}(\gamma)=A^{-1}\Phi_b(\gamma)A. \end{equation*}
    For \(p\in E\rtimes J\) define
    \begin{equation} r_A(p)= \kappa_J(A^{-1},A) +\kappa_J(A^{-1},p) +\kappa_J(A^{-1}p,A).\tag{A.35} \end{equation}
    This is the central coordinate of conjugation by the canonical lift of \(A\) in the extension represented by \(\kappa_J\text{.}\) Therefore
    \begin{equation} \nu_{\alpha,\widetilde s'}-\nu_{\alpha,\widetilde s} =d\bigl(r_A\circ\Phi_b\bigr).\tag{A.36} \end{equation}
    This displays the normalized boundary asserted after (6.40).

    Subsection A.8 The index-two specialization and the Evens normalization

    Let \(J=\langle s\rangle\cong C_2\) act on \(E_2=\F_2e_1\oplus\F_2e_s\) by swapping the two coordinates. Put
    \begin{equation*} q_{\mathrm{hyp}}(\notnfar{x1}{x_1},x_s)=x_1x_s, \qquad f_J(x,y)=x_1y_s, \qquad m_1=0, \qquad m_s(y)=y_1y_s, \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation} \kappa_J((x,c),(y,d))=f_J(x,cy)+m_c(y).\tag{A.37} \end{equation}
    The identities
    \begin{align*} m_s(v+w)+m_s(v)+m_s(w)&=f_J(sv,sw)+f_J(v,w),\\ m_s(sv)+m_s(v)&=0 \end{align*}
    show directly, after expanding \(d\kappa_J((x,c),(y,d),(z,e))\text{,}\) that \(d\kappa_J=0\text{.}\) The cochain is normalized. On the zero section it is zero, and on the fibre
    \begin{equation*} \kappa_J((x,1),(x,1))=f_J(x,x)=x_1x_s, \end{equation*}
    so its square map is \(q_{\mathrm{hyp}}\text{.}\) The lifts of \(e_1,e_s\) are involutions, their commutator is the central involution, and their product has nontrivial square; the fibre extension is therefore \(D_8\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We normalize the homogeneous index-two Evens norm by requiring
    \begin{equation} N_N^G(1+[\alpha]) =1+\operatorname{cor}_N^G[\alpha] +N_N^{G,\mathrm{Ev}}([\alpha])\tag{A.38} \end{equation}
    for \([\alpha]\in H^1(N,\F_2)\text{.}\) For the universal inclusion \(E_2\leq E_2\rtimes J\text{,}\) Mackey restriction gives
    \begin{equation*} \operatorname{res}_{E_2} N_{E_2}^{E_2\rtimes J,\mathrm{Ev}}(e_1^\vee) =e_1^\vee e_s^\vee, \qquad \operatorname{res}_J N_{E_2}^{E_2\rtimes J,\mathrm{Ev}}(e_1^\vee)=0. \end{equation*}
    Since
    \begin{equation*} H^1(J,E_2^\vee)=\ker(1+s)/\operatorname{im}(1+s)=0, \end{equation*}
    the low-degree sequence for \(1\to E_2\to E_2\rtimes J\to J\to1\) shows that these two restrictions determine the class. They are precisely the fibre and zero-section restrictions of (A.37); hence
    \begin{equation} [\kappa_J]=N_{E_2}^{E_2\rtimes J,\mathrm{Ev}}(e_1^\vee).\tag{A.39} \end{equation}
    🔗
    Now let \(N\triangleleft G\) have index two and let \(b=\operatorname{Sh}(\alpha)\) be the cocycle (A.12). By Subsection A.2, the graph \(\gamma\mapsto(b(\gamma),\bar\gamma)\) is a homomorphism. Pulling back (A.37) gives, literally,
    \begin{equation} \nu_\alpha(\gamma,\notnfar{eta}{\eta}) =b(\gamma)_1b(\notnfar{eta}{\eta})_{\bar\gamma^{-1}s} +\varepsilon(\bar\gamma)b(\notnfar{eta}{\eta})_1b(\notnfar{eta}{\eta})_s,\tag{A.40} \end{equation}
    where \(\varepsilon(1)=0\) and \(\varepsilon(s)=1\text{.}\) Naturality of the Evens norm in this pullback square and (A.39) give
    \begin{equation*} [\nu_\alpha]=N_N^{G,\mathrm{Ev}}([\alpha]). \end{equation*}
    For profinite Galois groups, every cochain in this construction factors through a finite quotient, and the displayed finite-group normalization is applied there.
    🔗
    The convention (A.38) is also the one used in the Evens–Kahn formula: for a quadratic extension \(L/k\text{,}\) the degree-two term in the relative total Stiefel–Whitney identity is
    \begin{equation*} w(\operatorname{Tr}_{L/k}\langle a\rangle) =w(\operatorname{Tr}_{L/k}\langle1\rangle) \bigl(1+\operatorname{cor}_{L/k}[a] +N_{L/k}^{\mathrm{Ev}}([a])\bigr). \end{equation*}
    Thus the normalization in this appendix is exactly the normalization used in (6.53); no additional sign or inverse convention enters Lemma 6.16.

    Appendix B Finite Fox–Heisenberg rules and word ledger

    We collect the finite word-evaluation rules underlying the first- and second-order deformation calculations. Direct multiplication in the relevant semidirect, Heisenberg, and extraspecial groups yields the Jacobian, mixed pairing, and candidate quadratic-form ledgers, while the finite-exponent argument shows that these calculations do not depend on the chosen integer representative of the profinite exponent.
    🔗
    This appendix starts from the finite semidirect, Heisenberg, and extraspecial groups used in the first- and second-order calculations. It records the literal word-evaluation identities that supply the Jacobian, mixed pairing, and candidate quadratic form in Section 5, Section 6.
    🔗
    The rules below are those used in Lemma 5.4, Lemma 5.5, Lemma 5.14, Proposition 6.5. They are identities of finite word evaluations before passage to cohomology; no formal Fox calculus over an infinite completed group is invoked. For each finite coefficient calculation one may replace \(\omega_2\) by any ordinary integer representative modulo the exponent of the finite semidirect, Heisenberg, or extraspecial group being evaluated; Lemma 5.1 then shows that the resulting first and mixed second derivatives are independent of the representative.
    🔗
    Let \(G\) act on \(A\) and \(A^\vee\text{,}\) and evaluate a word in
    \begin{equation*} G\ltimes(A\times A^\vee\times\F_2). \end{equation*}
    If \(\bar u\) is its base value, \(D_u,D_u^\vee\) are its two first derivatives, and \(\beta_u\) is its mixed central coordinate, then direct multiplication in the finite Heisenberg group gives
    \begin{align} D_{uv}&=D_u+\bar uD_v, &D_{u^{-1}}&=-\bar u^{-1}D_u,\tag{B.1}\\ D_{u^g}&=\bar g^{-1}D_u+\bar g^{-1}(\bar u-1)D_g, &D_{u^n}&=\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}\bar u^jD_u,\notag\\ \beta_{uv}&=\beta_u+\beta_v+(D_u^\vee)(\bar uD_v), &\beta_{u^{-1}}&=\beta_u+(\bar u^{-1}D_u^\vee)(\bar u^{-1}D_u).\tag{B.2} \end{align}
    The formulas for conjugates, commutators, and powers follow recursively. When \(\bar u\) has odd order \(e\) and the exponent is the \(2\)-primary idempotent, the power sum is the norm projector
    \begin{equation*} P=1+\bar u+\cdots+\bar u^{e-1}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    We now spell out the first-order ledger at a tame lower map. Write \(\mathsf S,\mathsf T\) for the lower actions of \(\notn{sigma}{\sigma},\notn{tau}{\tau}\text{,}\) put \(\mathsf U=\mathsf S^{\omega_2}\text{,}\) and let \((a,b,c,d)\) be the lift variables on \((\notn{sigma}{\sigma},\notn{tau}{\tau},\notn{x0}{x_0},\notnfar{x1}{x_1})\text{.}\) The \(\omega_2\)-power rule gives
    \begin{equation} D(u_i)=P(Dx_i+D\notn{tau}{\tau}),\tag{B.3} \end{equation}
    and hence
    \begin{equation} D(d_0)=P b+(P+1)c, \qquad D(z_0)=\mathsf U^{-1}c.\tag{B.4} \end{equation}
    The tame relator gives the unnormalized row
    \begin{equation} D(\notn{tau}{\tau}^\notn{sigma}{\sigma}\notn{tau}{\tau}^{-2}) =\mathsf S^{-1}(1+\mathsf T)a+(\mathsf S^{-1}+1+\mathsf T)b.\tag{B.5} \end{equation}
    The wild relator has the following first-order table:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|cccc} \toprule \text{factor} & a&b&c&d\\ \midrule h_0&0&0&0&0\\ u_1^{-1}&0&P&0&P\\ \notnfar{x1}{x_1}^\notn{sigma}{\sigma}&0&0&0&\mathsf S^{-1}\\ {[d_0,z_0]}&0&0&0&0\\ \bottomrule \end{array} \end{equation*}
    Thus
    \begin{equation} L_w=P b+(P+\mathsf S^{-1})d.\tag{B.6} \end{equation}
    Together, (B.5) and (B.6) are the Jacobian displayed in Lemma 5.5.
    🔗
    The only non-obvious entry in this ledger is the first line. The variation of \(g_0=\sigma_2^2\) contributes only in order at least two, because the lower values of \(\notn{x0}{x_0}\) and \(d_0\) at the tame map are both \(1\text{.}\) If \(P=0\text{,}\) then \(Dd_0=D\notn{x0}{x_0}=c\text{,}\) and the exact class-two identity of Lemma 5.2 shows that \(h_0\) has the same elementary and central shadow as \(\notn{x0}{x_0}^2\text{.}\) If \(P=1\text{,}\) then \(\mathsf T=1\) and the \(2\)-primary operator \(\mathsf U\) acts trivially on the simple characteristic-\(2\) quotient; its lift to the Heisenberg or extraspecial coefficient group is a central automorphism of order at most \(2\text{,}\) so \(g_0=\sigma_2^2\) acts trivially on the full coefficient group. Again Lemma 5.2 applies. This is the precise reason that \(h_0\) may be replaced by \(\notn{x0}{x_0}^2\) in the linear, mixed cup, and determinant calculations.
    🔗
    Write \(b_{\mathrm{cl}}\) for the bilinear commutator pairing of the chosen class-two coefficient group. For the mixed Heisenberg and extraspecial calculations on normalized representatives supported on \(\notn{x0}{x_0}\text{,}\) the ledger is
    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c} \toprule \text{factor} & V\text{-coordinate} & \text{central coordinate}\\ \midrule h_0&0&\lambda(c)\text{ in }H(A)\text{, or }q(c)\text{ for }\kappa_q^0\\ u_1^{-1}&0&0\\ \notnfar{x1}{x_1}^\notn{sigma}{\sigma}&0&0\\ d_0=(\notn{x0}{x_0}\notn{tau}{\tau})^{\omega_2}\notn{x0}{x_0}^{-1}&(P+1)c&*\\ z_0=\notn{x0}{x_0}^{\sigma_2}&\mathsf U^{-1}c&*\\ {[d_0,z_0]}&0&b_{\mathrm{cl}}((P+1)c,\mathsf U^{-1}c)\\ \bottomrule \end{array} \end{equation*}
    The starred central coordinates of \(d_0\) and \(z_0\) are immaterial because these words occur only inside the commutator. The table gives both the mixed Hessian in Lemma 5.14 and the exact base determinant word expansion
    \begin{equation*} Q_A^0(c)=q(c)+b_q((P+1)c,\mathsf U^{-1}c) \end{equation*}
    of Proposition 6.5. The starred entries may contain the non-diagonal action corrections \(m_c\) from Lemma 6.1; they are central coordinates of \(d_0\) and \(z_0\) and hence do not affect the commutator. No relator-by-relator Stokes identity is used: the correction terms in the finite-word Stokes formula cancel only after summing the tame and wild relators, exactly as in Proposition 5.8.
